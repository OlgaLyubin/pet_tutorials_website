Running
How much exercise, and the type of exercise your dog needs will largely depend on the individual dog’s habits, age and health. Some dogs will need loads of exercise throughout their lives compared to others which may be happy with the run of a garden and a daily walk. Your dog needs to be taught how to walk on a lead. In towns, a lead will protect him/her from traffic and keep him/her away from places where young children play. Dog faeces can be a health hazard, so you must always pick up and dispose of your dog’s poo! Even in the countryside, no matter how placid your dog is, use a lead when walking near livestock which can often be the target of attacks by dogs. But try to find a safe area where you can let your dog off the lead for a good run around.

Why does my Dog Bark?

It's important to feed your pet the right dog food and treats for its age and breed


Types
Dogs can bark for a variety of different reasons such as during play, as a greeting or for some attention. However, prolonged periods of barking may be a sign that your dog is unhappy. Therefore, it is vital that you find out why this is happening. If your dog barks a lot when they are left alone, your dog may be having difficulty coping. It’s important that you talk to your vet about this, who may suggest a referral to a clinical animal behaviourist who will diagnose the root cause of the problem and develop a treatment programme, such as reward based methods.

Dogs can be aggressive in response to unfolding events because they think that they are under some sort of threat, e.g. their personal safety or that their territory is threatened. Dogs communicate mainly through body language, having a wide range of non-aggressive signals and postures they exhibit to stop us doing what they don’t like, including: If these fail, dogs will start to become more aggressive! Following these steps can help calm your dog down: 1. Stop whatever you’re doing, your dog wants you to stop. 2. Stand sill, movement towards your dog may be interpreted as a threat, movement away may make him/her bold enough to bite 3. Stand still until the aggression stops, or walk slowly backwards or sideways looking down 4. Talk to your dog reassuringly. 5. Get some professional advice if it continues!
